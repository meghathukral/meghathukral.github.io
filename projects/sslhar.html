How Much Unlabeled Data is Really Needed for Effective Self-Supervised Human Activity Recognition?

Sourish Gunesh Dhekane, Harish Haresamudram, Megha Thukral, Thomas Plötz
Proceedings of the 2023 ACM International Symposium on Wearable Computers (ISWC), Cancun, Mexico
Paper (DOI)
The problem. Self-supervised pretraining for human activity recognition has become standard practice, with pipelines that consume hundreds of millions of unlabeled sensor windows. But the field has not asked, with any seriousness, whether all of that pretraining data is actually pulling its weight. If half of it could be discarded at no cost, then practitioners are needlessly burning compute and storage.

Idea

We empirically study the impact of unlabeled data scale on self-supervised human activity recognition. The study sweeps the size of the unlabeled pretraining corpus across orders of magnitude, fixes the downstream evaluation protocol, and measures how much downstream HAR performance actually improves as more pretraining data is added.

What we find

Returns flatten earlier than the field has assumed. Beyond a moderate amount of carefully chosen unlabeled data, additional pretraining data offers diminishing returns for downstream HAR. The exact crossover depends on the SSL objective, but the qualitative pattern is consistent. This gives practitioners a defensible answer to a question they often ask in private: how much unlabeled HAR data is enough?
